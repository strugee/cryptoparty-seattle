Privacy basics (I) - some basic terms

Let's keep this short.

Encryption is the process of using a key, like a word or a number, to scramble some text so that it can't be read unless you have the key

Decryption is the process of using that same key to unscramble the encrypted text

Plaintext is text that has not been encrypted

Cryptography is the study and practice of encryption

We encrypt things to protect our privacy

Tor

What is Tor?

You know in those old movies where some kid is breaking into a computer and it shows the map, with the connection bouncing all around the world?

That's basically what Tor is.

Tor anonymizes you by making it seem like your requests for web pages are coming from a random location in the world

Tor can also help you circumvent censorship

Using Tor

Tor isn't useful on its own

Instead, you use your normal applications - like email, chat and your web browser - and tell them to send all their data through the Tor network instead of the regular Internet.

For your web browser, you use the Tor Browser Bundle

For other applications, you use their proxy settings

For BitTorrent, you do nothing. Don't use BitTorrent with Tor.

For the truly paranoid, you use Tails

The Tor Browser Bundle

TBB is a browser, based on Firefox, that is preconfigured to use Tor properly

It will help you connect to the Tor network and remain anonymous online

However, TBB cannot do everything. You may need to change some of your browsing habits.

What you need to do

Don't use browser plugins

Use HTTPS

Don't open downloaded documents while online

Be aware of logging in to websites (there's no point being anonymous if you tell them who you are)

Tails, The Amnesiac Incognito Live System

Tails is like the TBB, but it works for the entire operating system.

Instead of starting Tails from your hard drive, you put Tails on a CD and start the computer from the CD.

Nothing in Tails ever gets written to your hard disk unless you tell it to. When you shut down Tails, it forgets about everything you did.

It is advisable to start fresh when moving on to the next task by restarting Tails.

The warning about signing into things still applies, but otherwise, Tails is pretty hassle-free. Everything goes through Tor.

Getting Tor

Download the Tor Browser Bundle and unpack to your desktop. It is self-contained, no need to install

If you are paranoid you may wish to verify its signature to ensure it hasn't been tampered with

Download Tails from here and put it on a USB drive or a CD. Be aware of what it does not do for you.

For Android, you can get Orbot from the Google Play Store or from F-Droid. F-Droid is recommended to ensure it hasn't been tampered with.

The Tor wiki has a section detailing how to get and use Tor with other software.

Privacy basics (II) - public-key cryptography

Public key? Shouldn't I keep my keys secret?

Public-key cryptography refers to encryption systems in which instead of having one key, you have two.

This is useful because then you don't have to give the same key to all your friends.

(Imagine you used the key "asdf". You use "asdf" to encrypt emails to Alice and Mallory. Eve can't read your email to Alice, but now Mallory can, because you use the same key for both!)

Your two keys

In public-key cryptography, you have two keys. One private (duh), and one public.

When you encrypt something using your private key, it can only be decrypted using your public key. Your private key won't work for decryption.

And when someone encrypts something using your public key, it can only be decrypted using your private key.

There's a whole bunch of math that makes this work. You don't need to worry about that.

Things that public-key cryptography does

By encrypting something with your private key, you have digitally signed it

To verify the signature, someone else decrypts it with your public key.

By encrypting something with another person's public key, you can be sure that no one but them can read it.

Off-The-Record

Off-The-Record basics

Off-The-Record, also known as OTR, takes public-key cryptography and sticks it in chat.

It encrypts your chat messages so that no one, not even the organization who provides the chat, can read your conversations.

The tech

Luckily for you, you don't have to worry about keys when using OTR. OTR will manage them for you.

You just turn it on, challenge the other person with a question that only they would know (to make sure that it's really them), and talk

One caveat: OTR only works after the first message. Normally this probably isn't a big deal, unless you are e.g. whistleblowing.

So how do I use it?

Get Pidgin or Adium (for Macs) and turn on the OTR plugin.

Start a chat and click on the OTR icon. Follow the prompts.

Congrats! Your conversation is now encrypted.

Obligatory warning

If you are a highly-targeted individual, or are especially paranoid, do not use Pidgin.

Your risks are different and you must act accordingly.

Talk to me afterwards and I can explain why.

Pretty Good Privacy using GNU Privacy Guard

Some background

Originally, PGP referred to two things - PGP the system, and PGP the software

PGP the software was used to make use of PGP the system

Nowadays, we don't really use PGP the software. We use the GNU Privacy Guard instead.

GNU Privacy Guard

Normally called GPG and occasionally called GnuPG

GPG is used to access PGP the system, just like PGP the software.

GPG is different and better than PGP the software in several ways. Most notably, it is free software.

You don't really need to worry about this. GPG, PGP and OpenPGP (another name for PGP the system) are used interchangeably.

PGP the system

Usually known as the Web of Trust

The Web of Trust is the most difficult to explain of everything here

I will go slow and do my best to explain. Please stop me right away if I'm not making sense.

Web of Trust basics

Essentially, the Web of Trust is a way to authenticate who someone else is.

Put another way, the Web of Trust allows you to verify someone else's identity.

This works by constructing a chain of trust.

Trust chains

Trust chains start with who you trust.

Then they go to whoever is trusted by the people you trust.

GPG will construct these chains until you eventually get to the person whose identity you're trying to verify.

Trust chain example

A while back, I met up with Alice in real life, and I verified that her online identity matches her real life identity.

Now I can trust her online identity, because I've verified that it corresponds to a real person - and more importantly, the correct person.

Alice has met up with several people in real life, and done the same verification. Bob is one of the people Alice has met up with.

I'm trying to securely contact Joe. Bob has met up with Joe and verified his identity.

Because I trust Alice, Alice trusts Bob, and Bob trusts Joe, I can trust Joe.

From the conceptual to the practical

When I say "online identity", what I am really talking about is a public key

On a technical level, the Web of Trust is composed of two things:

  1. A collection of public keys
  2. A collection of signatures on those public keys

Keysigning

Every key in the Web of Trust claims to be owned by a real person. That claim may or may not be true.

In other words, when someone creates their key and fills in their name, they are either lying or not lying.

The act of signing a key says that you have verified, somehow, that the claim made by a key is the truth.

Usually, this verification is done by meeting up in real life and comparing government-issued photo ID.

If you know the person, and know what their voice sounds like, having them read their key ID to you over the phone will also work.

Why is keysigning valuable?

You want others to sign your keys so that people will know that your key is really yours

You want to sign others' keys so that GPG is able to construct trust chains to people you haven't met up with yet

You also should sign others' keys so that you don't appear rude, so that they will sign your key

A small note on how well you need to know the person

You do not need to know a person at all to sign their key.

You can even sign the key of a person you've just met. And, in fact, many people do at keysigning parties.

Just be aware of how you need to verify their identity. (I will talk about this in just a second.)

But wait, I hear you say!

What if I sign the key of a malicious actor? What if someone whose key I've signed signs others' keys without verifying their identity?

No problem. GPG will actually build more than one trust chain.

It requires a certain amount of chains, not just one chain, to have checked out before it will trust a key.

Keysigning best practices: for people you do know

This is the easy part.

For people you know well, a phone call will suffice. This is because you will recognize their voice - no (regular) texting!

You can skip the phone call if you have already established a secure conversation with them in some other way. For example, if you've verified their identify over OTR, you can use OTR to send them your key.

Keysigning best practices: for people you don't know

For people you don't know as well, it's not nearly as convenient. :(

At minimum you must require at least one form of government-issued photo identification. A drivers license will work, as well as a passport.

If you want to be really paranoid, require two forms of ID. But for the casual user, one will suffice.

Creating a key

The defaults are good

Don't enter anything in the Comment field

Generate a revocation certificate

Back up your key and your revocation certificate

Never, ever give your key to any person or company, even if it has a password. If a website asks you to upload your key, be suspicious.

Getting GPG

For Windows, get Gpg4Win (for the paranoid, verify the downloads)

For Mac OS X, get GpgTools (for the paranoid, verify the SHA1 hash. Talk to me afterwards.)

For pretty much everything on GNU/Linux, you want to use GNOME Keyring. This is probably already installed; if not, use your package manager's software installation tool to install it.

For KDE/Trinity, use either Kleopatra or KGpg.

Using GPG

If you use your Mac's builtin Mail application, then GpgTools has provided a plugin for using GPG.

For pretty much everyone else, I recommend using Mozilla Thunderbird with Enigmail.

For some GNU/Linux systems, Thunderbird may be called Icedove. It works the exact same way.

If you use Gmail, keep an eye on Google's End-to-End - it's not ready yet, but you can start using it when it's in the Chrome Web Store.

In the mean time, use Thunderbird.
