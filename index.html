Privacy basics (I)

Let's keep this short.

Encryption is the process of using a key, like a word or a number, to scramble some text so that it can't be read unless you have the key

Decryption is the process of using that same key to unscramble the encrypted text

Plaintext is text that has not been encrypted

Cryptography is the study and practice of encryption

We encrypt things to protect our privacy

Your threat model

Much as I would like to, I can't magically tell you what's right

Basically, a threat model is what your adversary looks like

You need to figure out your own threat model and base decisions on it

For most people this isn't a big deal

Activists, however, should pay special attention.

Tor

What is Tor?

You know in those old movies where some kid is breaking into a computer and it shows the map, with the connection bouncing all around the world?

That's basically what Tor is.

Tor anonymizes you by making it seem like your requests for web pages are coming from a random location in the world

Tor can also help you circumvent censorship

Using Tor

Tor isn't useful on its own

Instead, you use your normal applications - like email, chat and your web browser - and tell them to send all their data through the Tor network instead of the regular Internet.

For your web browser, you use the Tor Browser Bundle

For other applications, you use their proxy settings

For BitTorrent, you do nothing. Don't use BitTorrent with Tor.

For the truly paranoid, you use Tails

The Tor Browser Bundle

TBB is a browser, based on Firefox, that is preconfigured to use Tor properly

It will help you connect to the Tor network and remain anonymous online

However, TBB cannot do everything. You may need to change some of your browsing habits.

What you need to do

Don't use browser plugins

Don't open downloaded documents while online

Be aware of logging in to websites (there's no point being anonymous if you tell them who you are)

Tails, The Amnesiac Incognito Live System

Tails is like the TBB, but it works for the entire operating system.

Instead of starting Tails from your hard drive, you put Tails on a CD and start the computer from the CD.

When you shut down Tails, it forgets about everything you did.

Start fresh when moving on to the next task by restarting Tails.

The warning about signing into things still applies, but otherwise, Tails is pretty hassle-free. Everything goes through Tor.

Getting Tor

Download the Tor Browser Bundle and unpack to your desktop. It is self-contained, no need to install

If you are paranoid you may wish to verify its signature to ensure it hasn't been tampered with

Download Tails from here and put it on a USB drive or a CD. Be aware of what it does not do for you.

For Android, you can get Orbot from the Google Play Store or from F-Droid (recommended).

The Tor wiki has a section detailing how to get and use Tor with other software.

Off-The-Record

Off-The-Record basics

IM + privacy = Off-The-Record

OTR encrypts your chat messages so that no one read your conversations.

Not even the chat service.

The tech

OTR manages your keys for you.

You just turn it on, challenge the other person with a question that only they would know (to make sure that it's really them), and talk

One caveat: OTR only works after the first message. (Consult your threat model.)

So how do I use it?

Get Pidgin or Adium (for Macs) and turn on the OTR plugin.

Start a chat and click on the OTR icon. Follow the prompts.

Congrats! Your conversation is now encrypted.

Obligatory warning

If you are a highly-targeted individual, or are especially paranoid, do not use Pidgin.

Your risks are different and you must act accordingly.

Talk to me afterwards and I can explain why.

Some background

GPG = GnuPG = PGP = OpenPGP

PGP is used to protect emails

Web of Trust basics

The Web of Trust is something we use in PGP

Essentially, it's a way to distribute and authenticate keys.

Put another way, the Web of Trust allows you to retrieve and verify someone else's identity.

Keysigning

Keysigning: the act of creating a digital signature of someone else's key

You want to sign people's keys, to verify others' keys

You want others to sign your keys, so others can verify your key

Unless you have a problem with the government knowing your social graph.

A small note on how well you need to know the person

You do not need to know a person at all to sign their key.

You can even sign the key of a person you've just met. And, in fact, many people do at keysigning parties.

Just be aware of how you need to verify their identity.

Keysigning best practices: for people you do know

This is the easy part.

A phone call will suffice. This is because you will recognize their voice - no (regular) texting!

You can skip the phone call if you have already established a secure conversation with them in some other way.

For example, if you've verified their identify over OTR, you can use OTR to send them your key.

Keysigning best practices: for people you don't know

This isn't nearly as convenient. :(

At minimum you must require at least one form of government-issued photo identification. A drivers license will work, as well as a passport.

Compare photo ID to them, and compare the name on the ID to the name on the key

If possible, send your signature to them instead of uploading it.

(If you're using GNU/Linux, you can use caff.)

Creating a key

The defaults are good

Don't enter anything in the Comment field

Generate a revocation certificate

Back up your key and your revocation certificate

Never, ever give your key to any person or company, even if it has a password. If a website asks you to upload your key, be suspicious.

Getting GPG

For Windows, get Gpg4Win

For Mac OS X, get GpgTools

For pretty much everything on GNU/Linux, you want to use GNOME Keyring. This is probably already installed; if not, use your package manager's software installation tool to install it.

For KDE/Trinity, use either Kleopatra or KGpg.

Using GPG

If you use your Mac's builtin Mail application, then GpgTools has provided a plugin for using GPG.

For pretty much everyone else, I recommend using Mozilla Thunderbird with Enigmail.

For some GNU/Linux systems, Thunderbird may be called Icedove. It works the exact same way.
